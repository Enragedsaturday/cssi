# SPEC — Entrapment
slug: `entrapment` · bucket: `defenses` · status: NEW · template: Rule · Key cases (table: case · holding · weight · CL link) · Nuances & limits · Common pitfalls · Visual (Mermaid if multi-factor) · Flashcards (2-5 ::) · Sources

## Scope
A SUBSTANTIVE DEFENSE (not a suppression doctrine — labeled as such). The federal test is SUBJECTIVE: focuses on the defendant's PREDISPOSITION to commit the crime, not merely on government inducement. Government may furnish opportunity; it may not implant criminal design in an otherwise-unwilling person.

  - **Sherman v. United States** — _PROGENY — inducement of an unwilling person; reaffirms subjective test_ · SCOTUS · `DECK`

  - **Hampton v. United States** — _LIMIT — no due-process bar where a predisposed defendant deals in government-supplied contraband_ · SCOTUS · `DECK`

## Circuit splits / federal-state to flag
  - federal SUBJECTIVE test vs the minority/state OBJECTIVE test — note the federal courts use subjective (predisposition); flag the objective test as a non-federal alternative, illustrative only

## Visual (Mermaid)
Entrapment (federal subjective): Government inducement + defendant NOT predisposed → entrapment. Predisposed (Jacobson: before inducement) → no defense, even if induced.

## Common pitfalls (must cover)
  - thinking inducement alone is entrapment (predisposition controls)
  - applying the objective test in federal court
  - confusing it with a 4A/suppression remedy — it is a defense to liability
