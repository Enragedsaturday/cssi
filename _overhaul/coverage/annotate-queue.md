## Cluster 2 — Border device forensic searches & digital over-retention / particularity

**Annotate on:** [[Border Searches]] (forensic-device nexus / reasonable-suspicion split, circuits named) and [[Plain View Doctrine]] (digital over-retention / computer-search particularity).

| Case | Circuit | Position (best knowledge — confirm at ingest) |
|---|---|---|
| **United States v. Aigbekaen** | 4th Cir. | Forensic device search at the border requires a nexus to the *type* of contraband the border-search power protects against (heightened nexus rule). |
| **United States v. Kolsuz** | 4th Cir. | Forensic (non-routine) device searches at the border require individualized suspicion; companion to Aigbekaen. |
| **Alasaad v. Mayorkas** | 1st Cir. | Neither reasonable suspicion nor a warrant is required for basic or advanced border device searches (lower-protection contra position). |
| **United States v. Ganias** | 2d Cir. | Government over-retention of imaged digital data beyond the warrant's scope raises 4A problems (digital-retention limit; en-banc resolved on good-faith). |
| **United States v. Burgess** | 10th Cir. | Computer-search particularity — how broadly forensic examiners may search seized digital media. |

**Annotation note for S6:** The headline border-device split is already carried by covered *Cotterman / Cano / Touset*; this cluster adds the **4th-Cir. nexus rule (Aigbekaen/Kolsuz)** vs. the **1st-Cir. no-suspicion rule (Alasaad)**, plus the digital-retention/particularity overlay (Ganias, Burgess). Circuits named; persuasive-only. Per Decision 2, ingest the 4th-Cir. nexus rule as a standalone page **only if** the user later designates it controlling-in-circuit.
