# Common Legal Terms

A working glossary of the Latin and legal terms an instructor will hit when reading opinions, briefs, and motions — each with a plain-English definition and a short concrete example. These are established legal definitions, not case holdings, so they're stated plainly without citations.

## Criminal-law terms

**Mens rea** — the guilty *mind*; the mental state (intent, knowledge, recklessness, negligence) the prosecution must prove for a crime.
*Example:* Walking out of a store with someone else's umbrella by honest mistake lacks the mens rea for theft — there was no intent to steal.

**Actus reus** — the guilty *act*; the physical conduct (or unlawful omission) that makes up the crime.
*Example:* Pulling the trigger is the actus reus of a shooting; the decision to do it is the mens rea. A crime generally needs both.

**Corpus delicti** — the "body of the crime"; proof that a crime actually occurred, separate from who did it. A confession alone usually isn't enough without it.
*Example:* Before a murder conviction, the state must show a death caused by a criminal act — the corpus delicti — not just the defendant's admission.

**Modus operandi (M.O.)** — a person's characteristic *method* of committing crimes; the recognizable pattern in how it's done.
*Example:* A burglar who always enters through a rear basement window and disables the same alarm model has a distinctive modus operandi linking the cases.

**Probable cause vs. reasonable suspicion** — two tiers of justification. **Probable cause** = a fair probability that a crime occurred or evidence is present (the standard for arrests and most warrants); **reasonable suspicion** = a lower, articulable belief that criminal activity is afoot (the standard for a brief investigative stop). See [[Terry Stops and Reasonable Suspicion]] and the [[Fourth Amendment Analysis Checklist]].
*Example:* Seeing someone repeatedly case parked cars may support reasonable suspicion to stop and ask questions; finding the stolen items on them gives probable cause to arrest.

## Evidence & procedure

**Fruit of the poisonous tree** — derivative evidence; evidence discovered *because of* an earlier illegal search or seizure. If the "tree" (the original act) is tainted, its "fruit" is generally suppressed too. See [[Two Definitions of Search]] and [[The Exclusionary Rule]].
*Example:* An illegal stop reveals a key; the key opens a locker full of contraband. The contraband is fruit of the poisonous tree and may be excluded.

**Suppression hearing** — a pre-trial hearing where the judge decides whether evidence was obtained lawfully and, if not, whether to exclude it. See [[The Exclusionary Rule]].
*Example:* The defense moves to suppress a firearm found during a warrantless car search; at the suppression hearing the court rules whether the automobile exception applied.

**Motion in limine** — a pre-trial motion asking the court to rule certain evidence in or out *before* it's mentioned in front of the jury, to avoid prejudice.
*Example:* The prosecution files a motion in limine to bar any reference to the victim's prior unrelated arrests.

**Statement of facts** — the section of a brief or opinion that lays out *what happened*, in narrative form, before any legal argument.
*Example:* A suppression motion opens with a statement of facts: the time of the stop, what the officer observed, and what was said and found.

**Subpoena duces tecum** — a subpoena commanding a person to produce *documents or things* (duces tecum = "bring with you").
*Example:* A subpoena duces tecum orders a bank to produce a defendant's account records for a specific date range.

**Subpoena ad testificandum** — a subpoena commanding a person to appear and *testify* (the ordinary "show up and answer questions" subpoena).
*Example:* The arresting officer receives a subpoena ad testificandum to testify at the suppression hearing.

**In forma pauperis (IFP)** — "in the manner of a pauper"; permission to proceed in court without paying the usual fees because the party can't afford them.
*Example:* An indigent defendant files an appeal in forma pauperis, so the filing fee is waived.

**Statute of limitations** — the deadline by which a charge or claim must be brought; once it runs, the case is generally time-barred.
*Example:* If the statute of limitations on a particular fraud is five years, charges filed in year six are barred even with strong evidence.

**Voir dire** — "to speak the truth"; the questioning of prospective jurors (or sometimes a witness's qualifications) to test for bias or competence.
*Example:* During voir dire, the judge dismisses a juror who admits she couldn't be fair to anyone accused of the charged offense.

## Opinions & precedent

**Holding** — the court's actual *ruling* on the legal question presented — the binding part of the decision that resolves the case.
*Example:* The holding is that the warrantless search violated the Fourth Amendment, so the evidence is suppressed. That ruling on the legal question is the holding; supporting commentary not necessary to it is dicta.

**Dicta (obiter dictum)** — remarks in an opinion *not necessary* to the holding; persuasive commentary, but not binding.
*Example:* A court rules the stop lawful, then muses about how it *would* analyze a nighttime stop — that aside is dicta, not the holding.

**Majority opinion** — the opinion joined by more than half the panel; it states the holding and sets the binding rule.
*Example:* Five of nine Justices sign the majority opinion, which becomes the controlling precedent.

**Plurality opinion** — the opinion with the most votes when *no* single opinion commands a majority; its reasoning is influential but its precedential force is limited.
*Example:* A 4–1–4 split produces a plurality opinion that announces the judgment but leaves the governing rule unsettled.

**Concurring opinion** — an opinion agreeing with the *result* but for different or additional reasons.
*Example:* A judge concurs in suppressing the evidence but writes separately to say she'd reach it on narrower grounds.

**Dissenting opinion** — an opinion *disagreeing* with the majority's result; not binding, but it can shape future law.
*Example:* Two judges dissent, arguing the search fell within the automobile exception and the evidence should come in.

**Per curiam** — "by the court"; an unsigned opinion issued in the name of the whole court rather than a named author.
*Example:* The court resolves a straightforward issue with a brief per curiam opinion.

**Stare decisis** — "to stand by things decided"; the doctrine that courts follow precedent, which is what makes the court hierarchy work. See [[The Federal Court System]].
*Example:* A district court applies a controlling Supreme Court rule under stare decisis even though the judge personally questions it.

## Latin shorthand

**Habeas corpus** — "that you have the body"; a petition challenging the *legality of a person's detention* and demanding the custodian justify it.
*Example:* A prisoner files a writ of habeas corpus arguing he's held under an unconstitutional conviction and must be released.

**Ex parte** — "from one party"; a proceeding or order made with only *one side* present, without notice to the other.
*Example:* Police obtain a search warrant ex parte — the suspect isn't notified or heard before it issues.

**De minimis** — short for *de minimis non curat lex*, "the law doesn't concern itself with trifles"; too small or trivial to matter legally.
*Example:* A momentary, fleeting touch may be deemed de minimis and not rise to a legally significant seizure.

**De facto** vs. **de jure** — "in fact" vs. "in law." De facto = how things actually are in practice; de jure = how they are by formal legal right.
*Example:* A person who runs an operation without holding the title is the de facto manager; the de jure manager is whoever is named on paper.

**Prima facie** — "at first sight"; a showing strong enough to establish a fact or claim *unless rebutted*.
*Example:* Producing a valid signed contract makes a prima facie case that an agreement existed, shifting the burden to the other side.

**Pro bono** — short for *pro bono publico*, "for the public good"; legal work done *without charge*.
*Example:* An attorney takes an indigent appeal pro bono, charging no fee.

**Pro se** — "for oneself"; representing *yourself* in court without a lawyer.
*Example:* A defendant who waives counsel and argues his own suppression motion is proceeding pro se.

**Inter alia** — "among other things"; signals that a list is *not exhaustive*.
*Example:* The motion challenges the stop on Fourth Amendment grounds, inter alia — meaning other grounds are raised too.
