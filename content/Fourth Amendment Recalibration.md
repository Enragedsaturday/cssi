## Rule
The Fourth Amendment is not a fixed snapshot of 1791 — it is a **balance** between government power and individual privacy that the Supreme Court has had to **recalibrate** every time technology or society shifts the ground under it. Think of it as a doctrine that periodically needs a new release: a **"Fourth Amendment 2.0."** The balance is inherently unstable, because each generation produces new tools — the automobile, the telephone and the wiretap, the searchlight, the thermal imager, the smartphone, cell-site location data — that the existing doctrine never anticipated, and the Court must decide whether the old rule still fits. **Two engines drive the recalibration:** (1) **new surveillance and transport technology**, which sometimes *expands* government power (the automobile justified warrantless searches in *Carroll*; the wiretap escaped the Amendment entirely in *Olmstead*) and sometimes *contracts* it (privacy reclaimed the wiretap in *Katz*, the phone in *Riley*, location data in *Carpenter*); and (2) **incorporation against the states** — the Amendment going from a federal-only restraint to a fully state-binding one, the right incorporated in *Wolf* (1949) and the exclusionary remedy added in *Mapp* (1961). The throughline: the same constitutional text, re-fitted again and again to a changed world.

## Nuances & limits
- **The incorporation two-step — right first, remedy second.** It is half-right to say "until 1949 the Fourth Amendment only applied to the federal government." *Wolf v. Colorado*, 338 U.S. 25, 27–28, 33 (1949), incorporated the Amendment's **substance** against the states in 1949 — but it expressly **declined** to impose the federal exclusionary remedy. The exclusionary rule did not reach the states until *Mapp v. Ohio*, 367 U.S. 643, 654–57, 660 (1961). So: **1949 = the right; 1961 = the remedy.** Stating it as one event collapses twelve years of doctrine and the *Wolf*-to-*Mapp* overruling. See [[The Exclusionary Rule]].
- **Recalibration often means overruling.** *Olmstead* was good law for roughly **40 years** before *Katz* discarded its trespass theory in 1967. Recalibration is not always a gentle gloss — sometimes the Court simply concludes the old rule no longer fits and overrules it. *Katz*, 389 U.S. at 351–53; *id.* at 361 (Harlan, J., concurring) (the two-part reasonable-expectation-of-privacy test). The property/trespass theory was not erased, though — it was revived as an independent path in *United States v. Jones*, 565 U.S. 400 (2012). See [[Two Definitions of Search]].
- **The sense-enhancement thread: *Lee* → *Kyllo*.** *United States v. Lee*, 274 U.S. 559, 563 (1927), held a searchlight revealing what was in **open view** was not a search; *Kyllo v. United States*, 533 U.S. 27, 34–35, 40 (2001), held a thermal imager probing **through the wall of a home** with technology not in general public use *is* a search. Same principle — does the device merely improve ordinary perception, or does it pierce a space the Amendment protects? — recalibrated as the technology grew more invasive.
- **Direction is not one-way.** Technology has *expanded* government power (*Carroll*, *Olmstead*) and *contracted* it (*Katz*, *Riley*, *Carpenter*). The recalibration metaphor is about re-fitting the balance, not a steady march in either direction.

## Common pitfalls
- **Don't overstate *Carroll*.** The automobile exception still **requires probable cause**. *Carroll v. United States*, 267 U.S. 132, 149, 153–56 (1925). It is *not* a suspicion-free license to search any vehicle on a hunch — it dispenses with the *warrant*, not with the *cause*. See [[Automobile Exception]].
- **Cite the *Riley* merits, not a cert order.** The merits opinion is *Riley v. California*, **573 U.S. 373, 386, 393–403 (2014)** (Roberts, C.J., decided June 25, 2014; parallel cite 134 S. Ct. 2473). The cert-stage records (134 S. Ct. 999; 134 S. Ct. 1870) are **different clusters** — do not grab one of those for the holding.
- **Be precise on *Wolf* vs. *Mapp*.** *Wolf* incorporated the **right** (1949); *Mapp* incorporated the **remedy** (1961). Treating either case as doing both is a common error — see the two-step above.
- **Don't read *Olmstead* as still good law.** It governed for ~40 years but was **overruled** by *Katz* in 1967. Quoting *Olmstead* for the proposition that a wiretap is not a search states the *former* rule.
- **Modern cases are cross-links, not full holdings here.** *Kyllo*, *Riley*, and *Carpenter* appear on this page only as the modern beats of the arc — their full doctrine lives in [[The Third-Party Doctrine and Digital Surveillance]]. Don't re-litigate them in full on this conceptual page.
