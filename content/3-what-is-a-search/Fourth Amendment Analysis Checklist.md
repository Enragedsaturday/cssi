## Rule
A field-and-exam quick-reference for running any Fourth Amendment problem. Work the questions **in order** — if an earlier one fails, you never reach the next. This page is the checklist; the doctrine behind each step lives on the linked pages. The full decision tree is drawn on [[Fourth Amendment Framework]].

## The checklist
1. **Who acted — a government agent?** The Fourth Amendment restrains the government, not private parties. A private actor is bound only if the government instigated, directed, or significantly participated (state-action / private-search doctrine). → [[Fourth Amendment Framework]]
2. **Was a protected area or interest involved?** Persons, houses, papers, effects — **plus** any **reasonable expectation of privacy** (not limited to those four categories). The home's **curtilage** is protected; **open fields** and **abandoned** property are not. → [[Curtilage]] · [[Abandonment]]
3. **Did a search or a seizure actually occur?**
   - **Search** — a physical **trespass** on a protected area to obtain information, **or** an invasion of a **reasonable expectation of privacy**. → [[Two Definitions of Search]]
   - **Seizure of property** — meaningful interference with possessory interests.
   - **Seizure of a person** — physical force with intent to restrain, or submission to a show of authority. → [[Seizure of the Person]]
   - *If neither a search nor a seizure occurred, the analysis ends — there is nothing to justify.*
4. **Does this person have standing?** Fourth Amendment rights are personal — the claimant must show that **their own** protected interest was invaded, not a third party's. → [[Fourth Amendment Framework]] Note: *[[Rakas v. Illinois]]*, 439 U.S. 128, 138-40 (1978) abandoned 'standing' as a separate threshold inquiry and folded it into the merits — the question is simply whether the challenged search invaded the defendant's *own* Fourth Amendment rights/reasonable expectation of privacy (captured at step 2 here), not a distinct jurisdictional gate. Treat 'standing' as shorthand for that personal-rights merits question, not a separate step.
5. **Was it reasonable? → C.R.E.W.** **C**onsent, a **R**ecognized **E**xception, or a **W**arrant. No justification → presumptively unreasonable → suppress. → [[CREW]]

Steps 1–4 are the **threshold and standing** questions; step 5 is the **merits**. At every step, articulate the facts behind your call — use **"Strive for Five."** → [[Three Golden Rules]]

## Nuances & limits
- **Order matters.** Most suppression fights are won or lost at the threshold (steps 1–3) — if there was no government action, no protected interest, or no search/seizure, you never reach reasonableness. Don't jump straight to warrant exceptions.
- **Two theories of "search" run in parallel.** A trespass to gather information is a search *and* so is invading a reasonable expectation of privacy — either one is enough. → [[Two Definitions of Search]]
- **Standing is personal.** "The search was illegal" is not the same as "*this* defendant can suppress." Confirm whose protected interest was invaded. → [[Fourth Amendment Framework]]
- **C.R.E.W. is one step, not the whole test.** It answers only reasonableness, and only after the threshold and standing are satisfied. → [[CREW]]

## Common pitfalls
- **Skipping to C.R.E.W.** Justifying a search you never established was a search wastes the strongest argument (no Fourth Amendment event at all).
- **Asserting someone else's rights.** Standing requires the claimant's own interest. → [[Fourth Amendment Framework]]
- **Forgetting the private-search/state-action gate.** Evidence handed over by a genuinely private party is not a Fourth Amendment problem — until the government's involvement makes the private party its agent.
