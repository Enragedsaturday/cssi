# Common Legal Terms

A working glossary of the Latin and legal terms an instructor will hit when reading opinions, briefs, and motions — each with a plain-English definition and a short concrete example. These are established legal definitions, not case holdings, so they're stated plainly without citations.

## Criminal-law terms

### Mens rea
The guilty *mind*; the mental state (intent, knowledge, recklessness, negligence) the prosecution must prove for a crime.
*Example:* Walking out of a store with someone else's umbrella by honest mistake lacks the mens rea for theft — there was no intent to steal.

### Actus reus
The guilty *act*; the physical conduct (or unlawful omission) that makes up the crime.
*Example:* Pulling the trigger is the actus reus of a shooting; the decision to do it is the mens rea. A crime generally needs both.

### Corpus delicti
The "body of the crime"; proof that a crime actually occurred, separate from who did it. A confession alone usually isn't enough without it.
*Example:* Before a murder conviction, the state must show a death caused by a criminal act — the corpus delicti — not just the defendant's admission.

### Modus operandi
A person's characteristic *method* of committing crimes (abbreviated **M.O.**); the recognizable pattern in how it's done.
*Example:* A burglar who always enters through a rear basement window and disables the same alarm model has a distinctive modus operandi linking the cases.

### Probable cause
A fair probability — judged on the totality of the circumstances — that a crime has occurred or that evidence is present. It is the standard for arrests and most warrants: more than reasonable suspicion, well short of proof beyond a reasonable doubt. Full doctrine: [[Probable Cause and Reasonable Suspicion]].
*Example:* Finding the reported stolen items in a suspect's bag gives probable cause to arrest.

### Reasonable suspicion
A lower, *articulable* belief — more than a hunch — that criminal activity is afoot, justifying a brief investigative stop (and, where danger is articulable, a frisk). Full doctrine: [[Probable Cause and Reasonable Suspicion]] and [[Terry Stops and Reasonable Suspicion]].
*Example:* Watching someone repeatedly case parked cars can supply reasonable suspicion to stop and ask questions.

## Evidence & procedure

### Fruit of the poisonous tree
Derivative evidence; evidence discovered *because of* an earlier illegal search or seizure. If the "tree" (the original act) is tainted, its "fruit" is generally suppressed too. See [[Two Definitions of Search]] and [[The Exclusionary Rule]].
*Example:* An illegal stop reveals a key; the key opens a locker full of contraband. The contraband is fruit of the poisonous tree and may be excluded.

### Suppression hearing
A pre-trial hearing where the judge decides whether evidence was obtained lawfully and, if not, whether to exclude it. See [[The Exclusionary Rule]].
*Example:* The defense moves to suppress a firearm found during a warrantless car search; at the suppression hearing the court rules whether the automobile exception applied.

### Motion in limine
A pre-trial motion asking the court to rule certain evidence in or out *before* it's mentioned in front of the jury, to avoid prejudice.
*Example:* The prosecution files a motion in limine to bar any reference to the victim's prior unrelated arrests.

### Statement of facts
The section of a brief or opinion that lays out *what happened*, in narrative form, before any legal argument.
*Example:* A suppression motion opens with a statement of facts: the time of the stop, what the officer observed, and what was said and found.

### Subpoena duces tecum
A subpoena commanding a person to produce *documents or things* (*duces tecum* = "bring with you").
*Example:* A subpoena duces tecum orders a bank to produce a defendant's account records for a specific date range.

### Subpoena ad testificandum
A subpoena commanding a person to appear and *testify* (the ordinary "show up and answer questions" subpoena).
*Example:* The arresting officer receives a subpoena ad testificandum to testify at the suppression hearing.

### In forma pauperis
"In the manner of a pauper" (abbreviated **IFP**); permission to proceed in court without paying the usual fees because the party can't afford them.
*Example:* An indigent defendant files an appeal in forma pauperis, so the filing fee is waived.

### Statute of limitations
The deadline by which a charge or claim must be brought; once it runs, the case is generally time-barred.
*Example:* If the statute of limitations on a particular fraud is five years, charges filed in year six are barred even with strong evidence.

### Voir dire
"To speak the truth"; the questioning of prospective jurors (or sometimes a witness's qualifications) to test for bias or competence.
*Example:* During voir dire, the judge dismisses a juror who admits she couldn't be fair to anyone accused of the charged offense.

### Totality of the circumstances
A standard that weighs *all* the facts together, in context, rather than applying a rigid checklist or letting any single factor decide. It is the default lens for judging probable cause, reasonable suspicion, consent, and the voluntariness of a statement.
*Example:* No one fact was enough, but the late hour, the high-crime block, the suspect's flight, and the bulge at his waistband — taken together, under the totality of the circumstances — added up to reasonable suspicion.

## Opinions & precedent

### Holding
The court's actual *ruling* on the legal question presented — the binding part of the decision that resolves the case.
*Example:* The holding is that the warrantless search violated the Fourth Amendment, so the evidence is suppressed. That ruling on the legal question is the holding; supporting commentary not necessary to it is dicta.

### Dicta
Remarks in an opinion *not necessary* to the holding (singular *dictum*, also called *obiter dictum*); persuasive commentary, but not binding.
*Example:* A court rules the stop lawful, then muses about how it *would* analyze a nighttime stop — that aside is dicta, not the holding.

### Majority opinion
The opinion joined by more than half the panel; it states the holding and sets the binding rule.
*Example:* Five of nine Justices sign the majority opinion, which becomes the controlling precedent.

### Plurality opinion
The opinion with the most votes when *no* single opinion commands a majority; its reasoning is influential but its precedential force is limited.
*Example:* A 4–1–4 split produces a plurality opinion that announces the judgment but leaves the governing rule unsettled.

### Concurring opinion
An opinion agreeing with the *result* but for different or additional reasons.
*Example:* A judge concurs in suppressing the evidence but writes separately to say she'd reach it on narrower grounds.

### Dissenting opinion
An opinion *disagreeing* with the majority's result; not binding, but it can shape future law.
*Example:* Two judges dissent, arguing the search fell within the automobile exception and the evidence should come in.

### Per curiam
"By the court"; an unsigned opinion issued in the name of the whole court rather than a named author.
*Example:* The court resolves a straightforward issue with a brief per curiam opinion.

### Stare decisis
"To stand by things decided"; the doctrine that courts follow precedent, which is what makes the court hierarchy work. See [[The Federal Court System]].
*Example:* A district court applies a controlling Supreme Court rule under stare decisis even though the judge personally questions it.

### De novo
A standard of appellate review in which the higher court decides the question *fresh*, giving no deference to the lower court's answer — used for questions of law and ultimate legal conclusions (such as whether a set of facts adds up to reasonable suspicion). Contrast *clear-error* review of a trial court's fact-findings.
*Example:* On appeal, whether the officer's observations amounted to probable cause is reviewed de novo, while what the officer actually saw and heard is reviewed only for clear error.

### Clear error
The deferential standard for reviewing a trial court's *findings of historical fact*: the finding stands unless the reviewing court is left with a firm conviction that a mistake was made. Contrast *de novo* review of legal questions.
*Example:* Whether the officer actually saw the handoff is reviewed for clear error; whether that handoff amounted to probable cause is reviewed de novo.

## Latin shorthand

### Habeas corpus
"That you have the body"; a petition challenging the *legality of a person's detention* and demanding the custodian justify it.
*Example:* A prisoner files a writ of habeas corpus arguing he's held under an unconstitutional conviction and must be released.

### Ex parte
"From one party"; a proceeding or order made with only *one side* present, without notice to the other.
*Example:* Police obtain a search warrant ex parte — the suspect isn't notified or heard before it issues.

### De minimis
Short for *de minimis non curat lex*, "the law doesn't concern itself with trifles"; too small or trivial to matter legally.
*Example:* A momentary, fleeting touch may be deemed de minimis and not rise to a legally significant seizure.

### De facto
"In fact" — how things actually are in practice, regardless of formal legal right. Contrast *de jure*.
*Example:* A person who runs an operation without holding the title is the de facto manager.

### De jure
"In law" — how things stand by formal legal right or official designation, regardless of practice. Contrast *de facto*.
*Example:* The de jure manager is whoever is named on the paperwork, even if someone else really runs the place.

### Prima facie
"At first sight"; a showing strong enough to establish a fact or claim *unless rebutted*.
*Example:* Producing a valid signed contract makes a prima facie case that an agreement existed, shifting the burden to the other side.

### Pro bono
Short for *pro bono publico*, "for the public good"; legal work done *without charge*.
*Example:* An attorney takes an indigent appeal pro bono, charging no fee.

### Pro se
"For oneself"; representing *yourself* in court without a lawyer.
*Example:* A defendant who waives counsel and argues his own suppression motion is proceeding pro se.

### Inter alia
"Among other things"; signals that a list is *not exhaustive*.
*Example:* The motion challenges the stop on Fourth Amendment grounds, inter alia — meaning other grounds are raised too.

### Per se
"By itself" — describing a rule that applies automatically and categorically, without any case-by-case, fact-weighing inquiry. A *per se* rule is the opposite of a totality-of-the-circumstances test.
*Example:* A per se rule bars the evidence outright once the violation is shown, instead of asking whether, on these particular facts, it should still come in.

### Respondeat superior
"Let the superior answer"; the principle that an employer is liable for wrongful acts an employee commits within the scope of employment. Its limits in civil-rights suits against government bodies are treated on [[Section 1983 Liability and Qualified Immunity]].
*Example:* Under ordinary respondeat superior, a trucking company can be sued for a collision its driver causes while on the job.
