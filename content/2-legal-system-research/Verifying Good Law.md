**Before I teach or rely on this case — is it still good law?** Finding an opinion is only half the job; a case that's been narrowed, reversed, or overruled is a trap, not a rule. Lawyers run Shepard's or KeyCite for a red-flag. You don't have that budget — so here is how to **shepardize by hand** with free tools. This page closes the loop opened by [[Legal Research Tools]] (finding it) and [[Reading and Citing Cases]] (reading it).

## Run the citator by hand

Free tools don't hand you an editorial red flag, so you build the picture yourself from a **citation graph** — the list of every later case that cites yours:

- **CourtListener — "Cited By" / "Authorities."** On any opinion, *Cited By* lists later cases that cite it; *Authorities* lists what it relied on. This is a citation graph, **not** a Shepard's red-flag system — it tells you *who* cited the case, not an editor's verdict on whether it survived.
- **Google Scholar — "How cited."** The same idea, lighter: a panel of later citing cases with short quoted context.

Both show you the citing cases. **You** supply the judgment about what they did to your case.

## Scan the treatment

Open the most recent and most authoritative citing cases and read *how* they treated yours. Watch for a later court that:

- **followed** it (good — reinforced),
- **distinguished** it (limited to its facts — may not reach your situation),
- **criticized** or **questioned** it (weakening — read closely),
- **limited** or **narrowed** it (the rule is smaller than it looks), or
- **overruled**, **abrogated**, or **vacated** it (it's no longer good law — history only).

A **`vacated`** or **overruled** decision has been set aside — do not rely on it (see [[Reading and Citing Cases#vacated]]). A narrowed or reversed case is the classic trap: the language still reads well, but the holding no longer stands where you'd use it. Note the **date** of the latest treatment — a case is only "good as of" the last time you checked it.

## Read the opinion — never a headnote or summary

Headnotes, syllabi, and editorial summaries are **not the court's words** — they're a publisher's paraphrase, and they can subtly misstate the holding. Pull the **actual opinion**, find the language yourself, and **pin** to the page (or paragraph) it sits on. If you're quoting or attributing a holding, it comes from the opinion text, not a summary block.

## Confirm court, publication, and currency — then weight it

Before you teach from a case, confirm three things and then place its weight precisely:

1. **Which court** decided it — this sets the ceiling on how far it reaches (see [[The Federal Court System]]).
2. **Published / precedential** or not (an unpublished disposition is illustrative, not a rule — see [[Reading and Citing Cases#published-unpublished-and-per-curiam]]).
3. **Still current** — no overruling or abrogating decision has landed since.

When you state the weight, use the wiki's **six-tier authority lexicon** — never the vague "persuasive, not binding":

| Tier | What it means |
| --- | --- |
| **Binding — SCOTUS** | Supreme Court on federal law; binds every court. |
| **Binding in-circuit** | A circuit's published holding; binds the federal courts of that circuit only. |
| **Persuasive (outside circuit)** | A sister circuit's holding; carries weight elsewhere but doesn't control. |
| **Persuasive — state, illustrative** | A state-court decision used to illustrate a federal point. |
| **Persuasive only — non-precedential** | Unpublished / non-precedential dispositions. |
| **Historical** | Overruled, abrogated, or superseded — cite for history, not as law. |

And when the circuits **disagree**, **flag the split** — say the governing rule depends on where you stand and SCOTUS hasn't resolved it — rather than quietly picking the side you like ([[The Federal Court System]] explains why a split is a signal, not a rule).

## Save the unit

Record each verified case the way this wiki itself does, so it can be re-found and re-checked instantly:

- **Consistent Bluebook cite** — *Terry v. Ohio*, 392 U.S. 1 (1968).
- **CourtListener permalink** — the stable opinion URL, so anyone can open the exact text you read.
- **Pinpoint** — the page your point lives on, captured *when* you read it (`392 U.S. at 30`).
- A one-line note on the **holding**, its **weight** (in the six-tier lexicon), and its **good-law status as of** the date you checked.

That triple — **cite + CourtListener link + pin**, dated — is the unit of trustworthy legal research, and it's exactly what every case page and the [[Case Index]] carries. For a worked example of the search-and-verify loop, see the [[Case Index]] and any doctrine page's Key cases table.
